Chapter 4: Ecosystems (Complete Notes)

4.1: Habitats, Interactions & The Sonoran Desert

Core Concept: Understanding the fundamental building blocks of an ecosystem, focusing on habitats and the interactions between organisms.

IGCSE Key Definitions (Memorize these!):

  • Species: A group of organisms with similar characteristics that can interbreed to produce fertile offspring.
  • Population: All the organisms of one specific species living in the same area at the same time.
  • Community: All the different populations of different species living and interacting in the same area.
  • Habitat: The specific physical environment or place where an organism lives.
  • Ecosystem: A self-contained, interacting system made up of a community of living organisms (biotic) and their non-living physical environment (abiotic).

System Variables: Ecosystems are governed by two main factors:

  • Biotic Factors (Living): Predation, competition for mates/food, disease, and grazing.
  • Abiotic Factors (Non-living): Light intensity, temperature, water availability, soil pH, and oxygen concentration.

Case Study: The Sonoran Desert. This is a classic example of a unique, fragile ecosystem. Due to extreme abiotic factors (low water, high heat), species are highly specialized. By looking at the interactions, scientists can predict how the loss of just one node in this network (e.g., a specific pollinating bat) could negatively cause a cascade failure affecting many other native cactus species.

4.2: Ecosystem Diversity & Energy Flow

Core Concept: Recognizing how energy is captured and transferred through highly complex networks (Food Webs).

Trophic Levels (Feeding Positions):

  • 1. Producers: Usually plants. They convert light energy into chemical energy via photosynthesis. They are the energy source for the whole system.
  • 2. Primary Consumers: Herbivores that eat the producers.
  • 3. Secondary/Tertiary Consumers: Carnivores/Predators that eat other consumers.
  • 4. Decomposers: Bacteria and fungi that break down dead organic matter, recycling nutrients (but not energy) back into the soil.

Food Chains vs. Food Webs: A food chain is a simple linear diagram showing energy flow (arrows mean "is eaten by" or "energy transfers to"). A food web is a network of interconnected food chains, showing the complex reality of an ecosystem.

Pyramid of Numbers:

Shows the total number of individual organisms at each trophic level. (Note: Can be inverted! E.g., 1 giant Oak Tree feeds 10,000 tiny caterpillars).

Pyramid of Biomass:

Shows the total dry mass of organisms at each level. This is always a true pyramid shape because mass/energy is always lost as you go up.

4.3: Intruders & Competition Dynamics

Core Concept: Understanding the severe impact of introducing new variables (invasive species) into a naturally balanced system.

The Physics of Competition: Ecosystems have a limited carrying capacity (limited resources). When an intruder arrives, it alters the dynamic equilibrium. This leads to intense competition:

  • Intraspecific Competition: Competition between individuals of the same species (e.g., two lions fighting for territory).
  • Interspecific Competition: Competition between different species (e.g., invasive Buffelgrass competing with native desert shrubs for water). Invasive species usually win because they lack natural predators in the new environment.
Classic Exam Examples:
  • Resource Short-Circuit (Buffelgrass): An invasive plant that grows in dense patches. It has a structurally lower "resistance" to resource absorption, aggressively taking water and nutrients from the soil. This effectively starves the native desert plants.
  • Toxic Perturbation (Cane Toads): Introduced to Australia to eat beetles, they became a highly toxic invasive pest. Native predators (like the northern quoll) had no evolutionary immunity. Conservationists must feed quolls "cane toad sausages" to condition them to avoid eating the real, deadly toads.

4.4: Bioaccumulation & Energy Inefficiency

Core Concept: The thermodynamic inefficiency of ecosystems and how it leads to the dangerous magnification of indestructible toxins.

The 10% Rule (Why Energy is Lost):

As energy moves up the food chain, approximately 90% of the energy is lost at each stage. Only about 10% is stored as flesh/biomass for the next predator. This happens because:

  • Energy is dissipated as heat during cellular respiration.
  • Energy is used for life processes (movement, hunting, keeping warm).
  • Not all of the organism is eaten (bones, fur, roots).
  • Energy is lost through excretion (urine/feces).

This is why food chains rarely exceed 4 or 5 trophic levels—there simply isn't enough energy left to sustain another top predator!

Bioaccumulation vs Biomagnification:

  • Bioaccumulation: The build-up of a toxin in a single organism over its lifetime because the chemical cannot be broken down or excreted.
  • Biomagnification: Because energy/biomass shrinks by 90% at each level (due to the 10% rule), but the indestructible toxin mass remains constant, the concentration density of the toxin spikes drastically at the top of the food chain.
Pollutant Case Studies:
  • DDT: A pesticide that enters the water, is absorbed by plankton, eaten by fish, and magnifies in eagles, causing their eggshells to thin and break before hatching.
  • Microplastics: Pieces of plastic < 5mm. They enter the food web at the base and cannot be digested, filling up the stomachs of top predators.
